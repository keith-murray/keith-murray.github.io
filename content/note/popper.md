Digging through Princeton's library the other day, I found [this book](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9781139171434) titled *Proceedings of the International Colloquium in the Philosophy of Science* in 1965. That's a pretty boring title, so it was given a subtitle of *Criticism and the Growth of Knowledge*, which is significantly more interesting.

The context of the 1965 International Colloquium in the Philosophy of Science was that Thomas Kuhn's 1962 [book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Structure_of_Scientific_Revolutions) *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* had just circulated into mainstream scientific thought, catching the eyes of researchers outside of the philosophy of science community. Naturally, this did not please the community as there were many competing theories of scientific discovery and justification. Kuhn's book was itself staging a revolution in the philosophy of science.

*Criticism and the Growth of Knowledge* begins with a article by Kuhn entitled "Logic of Discovery or Psychology of Research?" where he defends his book's thesis against his main opponent Sir Karl Popper. Kuhn's book can interpreted as a counterargument towards Popper's own [wildly successful book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Logic_of_Scientific_Discovery) titled *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*. One can tell that the colloquium must have been intensely fierce given how Kuhn writes in the abstract of his article:
> [!quote]
> My object in these pages is to juxtapose the view of scientific development outlined in my book, *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*, with the better known views of our chairman, Sir Karl Popper. Ordinarily I should decline such an undertaking, for I am not so sanguine as Sir Karl about the utility of confrontations.

And Kuhn is right. Sir Karl is quite *sanguine* about "the utility of confrontations". In Popper's own article within *Criticism and the Growth of Knowledge*, titled "Normal Science and its Dangers", Sir Karl writes, referring to a quote from the *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* that Kuhn took out of context: 
> [!quote]
> The fact that Kuhn overlooked this point of agreement and that he fastened on what came immediately after, and what he thought was a point of disagreement, seems to me significant. It shows that one never reads or understands a book except with definite expectations in one's mind. This indeed may be regarded as one of the consequences of my thesis that *we approach everything in the light of a preconceived theory*. So also a book. As a consequence one is liable to pick out these things which one either likes or dislikes or which one wants for other reasons to find in the book; and so did Kuhn when reading my book.

Essentially, what Sir Karl is saying is that Kuhn failed to understand the point that Popper made and only extracted the message that Kuhn wanted to refute. Furthermore, Kuhn's misinterpretation underscores Popper's argument that scientists necessarily interpret data within the context of a scientific framework: Kuhn's misinterpretation was a result of Kuhn using his own philosophy of science to interpret Popper's book.

I have never seen such a brutal roast between two academics, let alone two professionals. It's a *meta-roast* on a level that Comedy Central could only dream of. I also like this quote because it underscores three important lessons:
1. I once heard [Eve Marder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eve_Marder) say at a conference, "In order to refute someone, you have to first explain their own idea to them so well that they say 'Oh man, I wish I could say that as good as you could!'" Many of the arguments that we experience happen because people bring different frameworks to the table. If we want any shot of resolving the argument, we have to understand the other side and their framework.
2. It's worth reading a book more than once. As we learn, our understanding of the world matures, and this allows us to see new data that we previously lacked the tools to understand. Therefore, if you like a book, find time to read it again in a few years.
3. Understanding our own assumptions is incomprehensibly difficult. Kuhn was a professional philosopher, and arguably one of the most influential of the twentieth century, and even he struggled to understand his own assumptions despite his research involving understanding the assumptions of scientists. That's [[note/irony.md | teleologically ironic]].
